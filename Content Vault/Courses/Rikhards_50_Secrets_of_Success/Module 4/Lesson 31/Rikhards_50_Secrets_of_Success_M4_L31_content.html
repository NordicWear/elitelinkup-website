Section 1: Only Partner With People That You Need - and Who Need You Let me make something clear from the start - business has no room for emotion. You can care about someone. You can love them. But once money and movement get involved, feelings become liabilities. I learned this the hard way. I had a friend I grew up with - six years old, same streets, same memories. We tried everything together: selling on Facebook Marketplace, flipping stuff from Alibaba, doing small sales gigs. Every time, I carried him. He was already deep into drugs, and every time I thought he’d change, I gave him another chance. People warned me. They said, “He’s going to fall back into it.” But I believed in him - not because he earned it, but because of history. And then one day, everything collapsed. He started accusing me of betraying him, saying crazy things on the phone like, “I know you’re trying to attack me.” That’s when I realized - you can’t help people who don’t want to help themselves. All I was doing was slowing myself down, dragging dead weight while trying to move fast. Here’s the truth: If someone doesn’t love themselves, they can’t be loyal to you. If they don’t respect their own grind, they’ll never respect yours. When you partner with someone who refuses to grow, their weakness eventually becomes your downfall. After I cut him off, everything got clearer. My focus, my time, my peace - all came back. I learned that loyalty isn’t about staying with people forever; it’s about aligning with people who move like you do. Now, don’t get it twisted - I’m not saying never have partners. Today, I have people around me who are disciplined, loyal, and sharp. Matter of fact, my best friend - the one I grew up with - he’s still here in my life. We’re both building, just on different lanes. No jealousy, no control, no competition - just mutual respect and power. We help each other, but we don’t depend on each other. That’s real partnership.

Because this is the rule: Only partner with people that you need - and who need you. Not want. Not like. Need. If you’re doing the same thing, one of you is redundant. If he’s not putting in effort while you sleep, he’s holding you back. This isn’t a friendship playground - it’s business. It’s war. You can keep your friends. You can love your family. But when it comes to business - only build with killers. Otherwise, build alone.

Section 2: Always Be Needed - Or Be Ready to Walk Alone Most people think partnerships are about trust. They’re not. They’re about leverage. If you don’t bring something irreplaceable to the table, the table will eat you alive. I knew a guy once - got screwed out of his own company. Three partners, each with thirty- three percent. Two of them woke up one morning, realized they didn’t need him anymore, and kicked him out. That’s business. Cold. Simple. The second they didn’t need him, he became disposable. That story stuck with me - because it’s not about betrayal. It’s about structure. If your role doesn’t make you essential, you’re just a placeholder. That’s why in every deal, every project, I make sure the people around me need me - and I need them. That’s balance. That’s safety. If your partners could replace you tomorrow, they eventually will. If you could replace them tomorrow, you probably should. The rule is simple: Mutual need keeps the engine running. Lack of need kills it. And don’t confuse “need” with “want.” People want good energy, friendship, connection - that’s nice. But in business, need is what keeps people loyal. They need your systems, your ideas, your presence - and you need their speed, their skill, their reliability. That’s how partnerships work when they work. When both sides are moving, building, producing - the energy multiplies. When one side stops, the other starts carrying - and everything falls apart.

So build like this: If you’re ever unsure whether to work with someone, ask one question - “Do they need?” If the answer is no, you already know what to do.

Section 3: Build Power Without Giving It Away Here’s what most people don’t get - You don’t need partners to build something massive. You just need the right structure and the right people under it. Most people want partners because they crave connection. They want someone to “vibe” with, to check their ideas, to make it feel less lonely. That’s not business - that’s therapy. If you’re built like me, you can get the same benefits of a partnership without losing control. You can hire someone. Train them. Make them your number two - loyal, sharp, ready to move. They get the responsibility. You keep the throne. That’s the secret. You can have the teamwork, the flow, the creative back-and-forth - but the final decision stays yours. Because once you give up control, you give up your freedom. And when freedom’s gone, everything else follows. If you still want a partner, that’s fine. But make sure you both need each other to grow. Not emotionally - strategically. You handle one lane. They handle another. They’re the weapon you don’t have, not the weight you have to carry. That’s how I move now. Every person around me has purpose, position, and power - but never permission to slow me down. If I disappear for a week, everything keeps running. If they disappear for a week, I feel it - because they matter. That’s balance. That’s control. So, remember: Build with people - not for people. Lead with logic - not loneliness.

Partnership isn’t about sharing power. It’s about multiplying it - without losing your throne.

Section 4: When to Cut the Rope Every empire falls the same way - too many people hanging on for the wrong reasons. You can’t move fast when you’re pulling dead weight. You’ll always feel it before it happens. The energy shifts. The messages slow down. The hunger fades. They start talking more than building. They start giving excuses that sound poetic but mean nothing. That’s when most people hesitate - “Maybe he’ll come around.” “He’s just going through something.” “We’ve been friends for years.” That hesitation is how empires die. Because here’s the rule: If someone’s not growing beside you, they’re rotting behind you. You can’t heal laziness with love. You can’t motivate someone who’s addicted to comfort. And you can’t run a business with someone who needs babysitting. When I see that shift - when I feel that a person’s stopped wanting it as much as me - I don’t argue, I don’t explain. I cut. No hate. No drama. Just a clean exit. I don’t lose sleep over it because I already know how that story ends - they’ll blame me, twist the narrative, play the victim. Let them. I’m not in business to prove I’m right; I’m in business to keep moving. Cutting someone off isn’t betrayal - it’s discipline. And the faster you do it, the faster you can refill that space with someone sharper, hungrier, and loyal to the mission, not the memory. So if you feel yourself making excuses for someone’s lack of movement, stop and ask - “Would I still hire this person if I met them today?” If the answer’s no, then the decision’s already made. Because every minute you waste dragging someone, you’re slowing down your own ascent. Cut the rope - or they’ll pull you down with them.

Section 5: Engineer Mutual Need - Not Emotional Dependence Partnerships don’t survive because of feelings. They survive because of function. Two people locked into the same mission, both bringing something the other one can’t replace. That’s what I mean by mutual need. It’s not “I like working with him.” It’s “If he stops showing up, my system slows down.” And vice versa - if I vanish, his whole operation shakes. That’s balance. That’s power. If you want a partnership that lasts, design it like a machine. Every part serves a purpose. Every gear moves something else. And if one piece stops, the whole engine feels it. You can’t have two of the same piece - that’s redundancy. You can’t have one weak link - that’s collapse. Your role should be your weapon. His role should be his. No overlap. No confusion. No competition. That’s how me and my closest people move now. My best friend - we’ve known each other since we were kids - he’s still in my circle. But we do separate things. We both grow, both win, and still help each other. No jealousy. No scoreboard. Just momentum. That’s what real brotherhood in business looks like. Not codependence - collaboration. Not comfort - contribution. If you want loyalty, don’t demand it. Design it. Make sure the system itself forces both of you to keep moving, keep producing, keep winning - because if one stops, both lose. That’s how you turn partnerships into power. That’s how you make sure no one ever needs to be carried again.

Because at the end of the day: Mutual need creates movement. Emotional dependence creates disaster.

Section 6: Be the System - Not Just a Part of It No matter how loyal your partners are, how clean your structure is, or how strong the movement gets - the truth stays the same: The empire lives or dies with you. You are the engine. The systems, the teams, the partnerships - they’re all extensions of your execution. If you stop, everything slows. If you move, everything multiplies. That’s why I never build something that can run without my awareness. I automate, I delegate - but I always know how every part works. Because control isn’t about doing everything; it’s about understanding everything. Too many people chase “freedom” but give away all their power to get it. They hand over the passwords, the systems, the decisions - and then wonder why they lose control. You don’t build freedom by escaping responsibility; you build it by mastering it. So even when I work with people I trust - even when they’re like brothers to me - I make sure I could still rebuild everything from scratch if I had to. That’s real power. That’s real freedom. Because here’s the rule: A king doesn’t depend on his castle. The castle depends on the king. Partnerships are useful. Systems are smart. But your movement has to be you-proof - built on skills, discipline, and awareness that no one can take away. If everyone around you disappeared tomorrow, could you still build again? If the answer is yes - you’ve already won. That’s the mindset. Build with people, but never because of people. Trust, but never rely. Love your allies, but never hand them your crown. Because the moment you give away control, you give away your kingdom. And I didn’t build all this to share a throne.

Final Section: The Circle That Moves While You Rest Here’s what this all comes down to: When you build partnerships on logic instead of feelings, you build freedom. Right now, I can take a vacation for months. Not because I quit working - but because I built a realistic system and a team that moves even when I’m gone. My partners and my people know their roles. They execute. The machine keeps running. If I’d stayed loyal to that old friend - the one lost in drugs and excuses - I’d still be stuck in Iceland, wasting years, surrounded by the same people who never changed. Still talking. Still dreaming. Still broke. But I cut that weight. I rebuilt my circle around discipline and movement. Now I’ve got millionaire friends, men who actually create, women who respect power, and a system that doesn’t need babysitting. And my real friends - the ones who grew with me - we’d walk through fire for each other. I’ll help them with anything. I’ll back them fully. But I don’t build my system around them - because it’s separate. If I don’t need them and they don’t need me, that’s the end of it. Period. That’s why everything runs smooth now. That’s why freedom is real. Because when you build the right partnerships, you can disappear for a while - and the world you created keeps growing without you. That’s what this lesson means: Build on logic, not emotion. Partner with power, not pity. And never be loyal to wrong people That’s how you move from survival to empire.
