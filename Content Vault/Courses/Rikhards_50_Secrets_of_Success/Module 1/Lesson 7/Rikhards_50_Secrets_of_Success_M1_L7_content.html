Section 1: The Core Truth - Why You Hire You don’t hire staff because it looks cool. You hire staff because you’re drowning. When you’re so deep in work that your hours start fighting back - that’s when you bring someone in. Not before. You hire for two reasons only: to outsource what kills your time or to bring in more sales. If you get a sales agent, he must make you more money than he makes himself. If you hire help, that help must buy back your time so you can grow. That’s it. Everything else is ego. Most people want a “team” just so they can feel like a boss. But you don’t get paid to feel like a boss - you get paid when the people around you move money or free time. Every hire should make the machine lighter or richer. If they don’t do either, you just hired your own delay.

Section 2: The Two Laws - The Only Two Reasons to Hire There are only two laws when it comes to building a team. Everything else is noise. Law One: The Sales Agent Law If you bring in a sales agent, he must make you more money than he makes himself. If he earns ten grand a month, your company should be pulling thirty. If not - he’s not a salesman, he’s a salary leak. You track his performance like a hawk. You know what he closes, what he costs, and what he’s worth. There’s no gray area - it’s math or myth. Law Two: The Outsourcing Law You only outsource to buy back time - not to feel busy, not to feel important. When you outsource, it’s because your calendar is bleeding and your brain needs space to scale. The purpose of outsourcing isn’t comfort. It’s capacity. If you’re handing off work, it’s so you can focus on what grows revenue, not what burns hours. And the person you outsource to must be trained to handle tasks through systems - not emotions, not opinions. Because once you start outsourcing, your only job becomes growth - not babysitting every source. If you’re still micromanaging, you didn’t hire an assistant - you hired a second version of your own chaos.

Section 3: The Pre-Money Mistake - You Don’t Need Anyone Yet I hear it all the time. “I need a VA.” “I need a designer.” “I need someone to help with my DMs.” No, you don’t. You don’t need staff. You need sales. If you haven’t made consistent money yet, you have no reason to hire anyone. You can’t outsource what doesn’t exist. There’s no task to delegate if the business isn’t breathing yet. Stop using “I need help” as a mask for “I don’t want to grind.” The truth is simple - until you’re generating income, every hire is a liability. You’re paying for motion that doesn’t multiply. Build first. Earn first. Pain first. Then, when the hours start breaking you and the messages stack up past midnight, that’s when you bring someone in. Not to fix your laziness - to extend your fire. Most people hire staff so they can avoid doing the real work. They want to look like they’re running something. But they’re running from effort, not running a business. If you’re watching TV at night and still think you need a staff - wrong. You don’t need an assistant while you’re still acting like one. You earn the right to hire only when your workload is proof that the system works.

Section 4: The Lazy World - What I Learned at Domino’s When I was working at Domino’s - broke, tired, like most of you are right now - I saw it every single day. People clocking in just to kill time. Five smoke breaks before lunch. Scrolling on their phones while the orders stacked up. Acting like they were exhausted after doing absolutely nothing. I was there, covered in sweat and grease, thinking, “This is what the world calls work?” You could’ve fired half that store, and nothing would’ve changed. The pizzas would still get made. The customers would still get fed. The only difference is there’d be less noise and more speed. That’s when it hit me - most people don’t go to work to produce. They go to survive the day. They move slow, talk fast, and get paid to exist. And if you bring that same mindset into your own business, you’ll end up hiring clones of the same people who used to drain your shifts. That’s why my rule is simple: every person I hire must either save me time or make me money. If they don’t, they’re gone. Because I’ve lived that broke life. I’ve worked next to the zombies. And I promised myself I’d never build a business that felt like those rooms again.

Section 5: The Flip-Era Story - When Outsourcing Became a Weapon When I started flipping facebook marketplace- phones, couches, sneakers, cars - I did everything myself. Every message, every pickup, every sale. My phone never stopped buzzing, and I never stopped moving. There were nights I’d fall asleep with my head on the keyboard, wake up to more DMs, and jump right back in. I wasn’t complaining - I was hungry. But I was drowning in motion. That’s when I learned the truth: you don’t outsource for comfort - you outsource for capacity. I didn’t bring people in until I was working through the night, until my hours were fighting back. And when I finally did, I didn’t hand them random tasks. I handed them systems. Every process had an SOP - step-by-step, no guessing. Every day had a tracker - what got done, what didn’t. Every person had a KPI - one number that proved they mattered. That’s the difference between hiring and scaling. Hiring is chaos. Scaling is control. Once I mastered it, things clicked. Outsourcing stopped feeling like babysitting and started feeling like cloning myself. I could trust the system to run while I chased bigger plays. And when I finally built systems that ran without me, everything changed. That’s the moment I had time to breathe - and for the first time, I didn’t use that time to rest. I used it to build something real. That’s how the marketing agency started. Not from luck, not from an idea - but from time I bought back through outsourcing. See, Facebook Marketplace was a hustle. Flip, sell, repeat.

Good money, no structure. I was always in motion, but motion without direction is just noise. Once I handed off the chaos - the DMs, the editing, the deliveries - I finally had space to think. And when you give a focused man time, he becomes dangerous. I used that time to study attention. To learn how money moves online. To build offers that scaled without needing me in every transaction. That’s when I realized - you don’t build a business by working harder. You build a business by buying time and spending it on bigger moves. If I never outsourced, I’d still be flipping couches and arguing with buyers in parking lots. I’d still be busy - but broke busy. Outsourcing didn’t make me lazy. It made me lethal. It gave me the power to shift from operator to owner, from worker to architect. And that’s the moment I stopped chasing sales - and started building systems that sell for me.

Section 6: The Movement Doctrine - System = Freedom Most people think freedom means doing nothing. They think being the boss means sleeping late, checking in once a week, and letting things “run themselves.” That’s why they never build anything that lasts. Freedom isn’t the absence of work. Freedom is the control of motion. When I finally built systems, I realized every successful business is just three things: SOPs, trackers, and KPIs. SOPs (Standard Operating Procedures) They’re the playbook. Every task has a script, a checklist, a repeatable flow. So when you bring someone in, they don’t “figure it out” - they execute. If you’re answering the same questions twice, you don’t have a system - you have noise. Daily Trackers Numbers don’t lie. Every person on your team should write down what they did, what they finished, and what result it created. Trackers turn invisible effort into visible truth. If you can’t measure it, it doesn’t exist. KPIs (Key Performance Indicators) This is where accountability lives. Every role has a number - one measurable output that defines whether they win or lose the day. No confusion. No debate. No guessing. These three things are what make a company feel fast, clean, and powerful. They turn your staff into extensions of you. They make sure your time is never wasted on correction - only creation.

People ask, “How do you trust your team?” I don’t. I trust my systems. Because systems don’t lie, sleep, or ask for weekends off. Systems don’t have moods. Systems don’t forget. The goal isn’t to work less - it’s to design a machine that works perfectly whether you’re online, offline, or halfway across the world. That’s real freedom. That’s how you step into the millionaire’s room with your eyes low-key on the business - because it’s not guesswork anymore. It’s mathematics.

Section 7: The Commandment Close - Audit + Ascend You’ve learned the rule. You’ve seen the pattern. Now comes the part most people never do - the audit. Go through every name on your payroll, every person you pay, every role in your circle. Ask one question: Do they save me time, or do they make me money? If the answer’s neither - cut fast. You’re not only firing a person. You’re freeing your future. Because dead weight doesn’t just slow your company - it kills your speed, your energy, and your hunger. You don’t owe loyalty to people who don’t move your mission. You owe loyalty to the version of you that’s still climbing. Once every hand around you is productive - once every system is tracked, every task is measured - that’s when you can finally step back. That’s when you enter the real millionaire’s room. Not because you’re rich - but because your time is. You’ll wake up when you want, travel when you want, and know that everything is still moving - not because of luck, but because of design. So write this down, burn it into your mind: If they don’t save you time or make you money - they slow your escape. Audit your world. Ascend through control. And never, ever hire for comfort. Hire for freedom. See you in the next lesson.
